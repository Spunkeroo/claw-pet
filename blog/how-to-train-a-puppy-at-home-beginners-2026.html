Before You Start: Essential Supplies and Mindset

Training a puppy is one of the most rewarding experiences a dog owner can have, but it requires patience, consistency, and realistic expectations. Puppies are not miniature adult dogs. They have short attention spans (typically 3 to 5 minutes for focused training), limited bladder control, a biological need to chew, and zero understanding of human rules when they first arrive in your home. Every behavior you want from your adult dog, from sitting calmly when guests arrive to walking politely on a leash, must be taught through repetition and positive reinforcement.

The single most important principle in modern dog training is positive reinforcement. This means rewarding behaviors you want (with treats, praise, play, or access to something the dog values) and ignoring or redirecting behaviors you do not want. Punishment-based methods, including yelling, hitting, alpha rolls, spray bottles, and choke or prong collars, are not only unnecessary but counterproductive. A landmark 2020 study published in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior compared dogs trained with reward-based methods versus aversive methods and found that dogs trained with positive reinforcement showed fewer stress-related behaviors, were more optimistic in cognitive tests, and learned new tasks faster. The American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior, the Association of Professional Dog Trainers, and virtually every major veterinary and behavioral organization endorses positive reinforcement as the most effective and humane training approach.

Before your puppy arrives, gather these essential supplies.

  • High-value training treats: Small, soft, smelly treats that your puppy can eat in one second. Zuke's Mini Naturals are a popular choice. You will use hundreds of treats during the first few weeks, so buy in bulk.
  • A properly sized crate: Large enough for your puppy to stand, turn around, and lie down. Many crates come with dividers so you can adjust the size as your puppy grows. Wire crates with a divider panel are the most versatile option.
  • Enzymatic cleaner: Essential for cleaning up house training accidents. Regular cleaners leave behind scent traces that encourage the puppy to eliminate in the same spot again. Enzymatic cleaners break down the proteins in urine and feces completely.
  • A 6-foot leash: A standard 6-foot nylon or leather leash provides enough length for walking while maintaining control. Avoid retractable leashes for training because they teach the puppy that pulling creates more freedom.
  • Chew toys: Puppies need to chew, especially during teething (3 to 7 months). Provide a variety of safe chew toys including Kongs, Nylabones, and rope toys. Rotating toys keeps them interesting.
  • A treat pouch: Worn on your belt, a treat pouch keeps rewards accessible for instant delivery during training. Speed of reward matters: the treat should arrive within 1 to 2 seconds of the desired behavior.

House Training (Potty Training)

House training is the first and most urgent training priority for every new puppy owner. A puppy's bladder control is directly related to their age. As a general rule, a puppy can hold their bladder for approximately their age in months plus one hour. A 2-month-old puppy can hold it for about 3 hours. A 4-month-old can hold it for about 5 hours. No puppy should be expected to hold their bladder for more than 6 to 8 hours, regardless of age.

The Basic Method

Take your puppy outside to the same designated spot at these key times: immediately after waking up (from naps and overnight sleep), within 5 to 10 minutes after eating or drinking, after play sessions, after being released from the crate, and every 1 to 2 hours in between for young puppies. Stand quietly in the designated spot and wait for the puppy to eliminate. The moment they finish, immediately mark the behavior with an enthusiastic "yes!" or a clicker, and give a high-value treat within 2 seconds. The timing is critical. The puppy must associate the reward with the act of eliminating outside, not with coming back inside or sitting down after.

When you cannot directly supervise your puppy indoors, they should be in their crate. This is not punishment. It is management. Puppies instinctively avoid eliminating in their sleeping space, so the crate leverages this natural behavior to prevent accidents. When you are supervising, keep the puppy in the same room as you and watch for signs they need to go: sniffing the floor in circles, squatting, moving toward the door, or becoming restless. At the first sign, immediately take them outside.

Handling Accidents

Accidents are inevitable. When they happen, do not punish the puppy. Do not rub their nose in it, yell, or show frustration. Puppies cannot connect punishment after the fact with the earlier accident. Punishment only teaches them to fear eliminating in your presence, which makes them hide to go inside rather than signaling to go outside. Instead, calmly clean the area with enzymatic cleaner and make a mental note to supervise more closely or take the puppy outside more frequently.

If you catch the puppy mid-accident, calmly interrupt with a quick "oops" and immediately take them outside to finish. If they finish outside, reward enthusiastically. Over time, the puppy learns that eliminating outside produces treats and praise while eliminating inside produces nothing.

Pro Tip: Keep a house training log for the first two weeks. Record every time your puppy eats, drinks, naps, eliminates outside (mark success), and has an accident (note the time and what happened before). Patterns will emerge quickly. You might discover that your puppy always needs to go 7 minutes after eating or that accidents happen at 2 PM when your attention drifts. Use the data to adjust your schedule.

Crate Training

A crate is one of the most valuable tools in puppy training. When introduced properly, the crate becomes your puppy's favorite spot, a secure den where they voluntarily rest, sleep, and feel safe. Crate training supports house training, prevents destructive behavior when unsupervised, makes travel safer, and prepares your dog for veterinary stays and boarding.

Choosing the Right Crate

The crate should be large enough for your puppy to stand at full height without their head touching the top, turn around completely, and lie down stretched out. For growing puppies, buy a crate sized for their expected adult size and use a divider panel to reduce the interior space to the appropriate size now. This prevents the puppy from using one end as a bathroom and the other as a bedroom, which undermines house training.

Introduction Process

Never force a puppy into the crate. The introduction should be gradual and entirely positive. Place the crate in a common area of the house with the door open. Toss high-value treats inside and let the puppy enter voluntarily to retrieve them. Feed meals inside the crate with the door open. Place a comfortable bed or blanket and a safe chew toy inside. Over several days, the puppy will begin choosing to enter the crate on their own.

Once the puppy is comfortable entering, begin closing the door briefly while they eat a treat or chew a toy. Start with just 10 seconds, then 30 seconds, then 1 minute, gradually increasing the duration. If the puppy whines, wait for a moment of quiet before opening the door. Opening the door during whining teaches the puppy that whining produces freedom. If the puppy is genuinely distressed (frantic barking, drooling, trying to escape), you have progressed too quickly. Go back to a shorter duration and build up more gradually.

Basic Commands: Sit, Stay, Come, Down, Leave It

Teaching basic commands builds communication between you and your puppy, provides mental stimulation, and establishes you as a reliable leader who rewards cooperation. Keep all training sessions short: 3 to 5 minutes, repeated 3 to 5 times throughout the day. Puppies learn more from multiple short sessions than one long session.

Sit

Hold a treat at your puppy's nose level. Slowly move the treat backward over their head. As their nose follows the treat upward, their rear will naturally lower to the ground. The instant their bottom touches the floor, say "yes!" and deliver the treat. Repeat 5 to 10 times. Once the puppy is sitting reliably when lured, add the verbal cue "sit" just before the luring motion. Within a few days, the puppy will respond to the verbal cue alone. Gradually fade the treat from your hand (use an empty hand motion, then reward from your pocket or treat pouch).

Come (Recall)

Recall is the single most important safety command. Start indoors in a low-distraction environment. Say your puppy's name followed by "come" in an enthusiastic, high-pitched voice. When they move toward you, reward with a high-value treat and genuine praise. Practice in hallways and between rooms. Never call your puppy to you for something unpleasant (bath, nail trimming, crating). Coming to you must always predict good things. As reliability builds indoors, practice in a fenced yard, then on a long line (15 to 30-foot leash) in more distracting environments. A solid recall takes months of consistent practice and can genuinely save your dog's life.

Stay

Ask your puppy to sit. Hold your palm out in a stop gesture and say "stay." Wait one second, then say "yes!" and treat. Gradually increase the duration: 2 seconds, 5 seconds, 10 seconds, 30 seconds. Then add distance: take one step back, return, and reward. Then two steps. Then three. If the puppy breaks the stay, calmly reset them and reduce the criteria. Do not repeat the cue. Say "stay" once and expect compliance. If the puppy cannot hold the stay, you have asked for too much too quickly.

Down

From a sitting position, hold a treat at your puppy's nose and slowly lower it straight down to the floor between their front paws. As their nose follows the treat, their body will fold into a down position. The instant their elbows touch the floor, mark "yes!" and treat. If the puppy stands up instead of lying down, try moving the treat more slowly or luring from a standing position by moving the treat down and slightly toward you. Add the verbal cue "down" once the puppy is following the lure reliably.

Leave It

Place a treat in your closed fist and present it to the puppy. They will sniff, lick, and paw at your hand. Wait. The moment they pull their nose away from your hand (even for a split second), say "yes!" and give them a different, better treat from your other hand. The puppy learns that ignoring the offered item produces a better reward. Gradually make it harder: place the treat on the floor covered by your hand, then uncovered but within reach, then at a distance. "Leave it" prevents your puppy from eating dangerous items like chocolate, medications, chicken bones, and toxic plants.

Bite Inhibition and Mouthing

Puppy biting is one of the most common complaints from new owners, and also one of the most misunderstood behaviors. Puppies explore the world with their mouths. They bite during play, when they are teething, when they are overstimulated, and when they are overtired. This is normal, developmentally appropriate behavior. The goal is not to stop all mouthing immediately but to teach bite inhibition: the ability to control the force of their bite.

The most effective method mimics what puppies learn from their littermates. When a puppy bites too hard during play with siblings, the bitten puppy yelps loudly and stops playing. This teaches the biter that too-hard biting ends the fun. You can replicate this process.

When your puppy bites you, immediately say "ouch!" in a sharp, high-pitched voice (not angry, just startled). Go completely still and withdraw all attention for 10 to 15 seconds. If the puppy calms down, resume interaction and redirect them to a chew toy. If they bite hard again, repeat the "ouch" and withdraw attention. If the puppy is too aroused to settle, leave the room entirely for 30 seconds. This teaches a clear lesson: gentle mouthing is tolerated, hard biting ends all fun immediately.

Teething peaks between 3 and 6 months of age when adult teeth are pushing through. During this period, provide plenty of appropriate chew outlets. Frozen Kongs stuffed with peanut butter and banana, frozen wet washcloths, rubber teething toys, and bully sticks all provide relief. Redirect biting from your hands and furniture to these appropriate items. A Kong Classic toy is the most versatile and durable option for teething puppies.

Socialization: The Critical Window

Socialization is the single most important aspect of raising a well-adjusted adult dog. The critical socialization window occurs between 3 and 16 weeks of age. During this period, puppies are naturally curious and accepting of new experiences. Positive exposure to a wide variety of people, animals, environments, sounds, surfaces, and situations during this window creates a confident, resilient adult dog. Lack of socialization during this period is the leading cause of fear-based aggression and anxiety in adult dogs.

The goal is not just exposure but positive exposure. Every new experience should be paired with treats, praise, and calm reassurance. If your puppy shows fear, do not force them closer to the scary stimulus. Instead, increase distance until they are comfortable, reward calm behavior, and try again more gradually later.

Socialization Checklist

  • People: Men, women, children of various ages, people wearing hats, sunglasses, uniforms, people with beards, people using wheelchairs or crutches, people of different ethnicities
  • Animals: Other puppies (in controlled puppy classes), vaccinated adult dogs (calm, friendly ones), cats (if applicable)
  • Surfaces: Grass, concrete, gravel, metal grates, carpet, tile, wood floors, wet surfaces
  • Sounds: Traffic, sirens, thunderstorms (recordings at low volume), vacuum cleaner, doorbell, fireworks (recordings), construction noise
  • Environments: Car rides, pet-friendly stores, outdoor cafes, different rooms in your home, friends' houses, the vet's office (for happy visits, not just when sick)
  • Handling: Touching ears, paws, tail, mouth, belly; brushing; nail touching (not trimming yet); gentle restraint; towel drying

Aim for your puppy to have positive experiences with at least 100 different people and dozens of new environments before 16 weeks of age. This sounds like a lot, but even brief, positive encounters count. A 5-second interaction with a friendly stranger who feeds your puppy a treat is a successful socialization experience.

Important Safety Note: Puppies are not fully vaccinated until approximately 16 weeks of age. During the socialization window, balance socialization needs with disease risk. Avoid dog parks, areas with heavy dog traffic, and unknown dogs until vaccinations are complete. Puppy socialization classes held on sanitized surfaces with verified vaccination records are safe and recommended by the American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior. Carry your puppy in public places rather than letting them walk on potentially contaminated ground.

Leash Training

Puppies are not born knowing how to walk on a leash. The first time you attach a leash, expect your puppy to pull, bite the leash, sit down and refuse to move, or spin in circles. This is normal. Leash training takes patience and consistency.

Start indoors. Let the puppy drag a lightweight leash around the house under supervision (never leave a leash on an unsupervised puppy due to entanglement risk). Once they are comfortable with the leash, pick up your end and follow the puppy around. Do not pull them in any direction yet. Just follow where they go while keeping the leash loose.

Next, teach the puppy to follow you. Hold a treat at your side and lure the puppy into walking beside you. Take a few steps, then stop and reward. Gradually increase the number of steps between rewards. If the puppy pulls ahead, stop walking immediately. Stand still like a tree until the leash goes slack, then resume walking. The puppy learns that a tight leash stops all forward progress while a loose leash allows continued walking. This takes weeks of consistent practice, but the result is a dog that walks pleasantly without pulling.

For puppies that pull excessively, a front-clip harness redirects their forward momentum to the side, making pulling mechanically less effective. Avoid using choke chains, prong collars, or shock collars on puppies. These tools cause pain and fear, damage the trust between you and your puppy, and can cause physical injury to developing bodies.

Feeding Schedule and Food Training

A consistent feeding schedule supports house training (predictable input means predictable output) and creates opportunities for training. Feed your puppy at the same times every day. Most puppies under 6 months should eat three meals per day. After 6 months, you can transition to two meals per day.

Use mealtimes as training opportunities. Ask your puppy to sit before placing the food bowl on the floor. This teaches impulse control and reinforces that good things come from cooperating with you. You can also use a portion of each meal as training treats throughout the day, ensuring your puppy does not gain excessive weight from treat-heavy training sessions.

Common Puppy Problems and Solutions

Chewing on furniture and shoes. Chewing is a biological need, not a behavior problem. Provide appropriate chew outlets (Kongs, bully sticks, Nylabones) and manage the environment by puppy-proofing your home. Keep shoes in closets, use bitter apple spray on furniture legs, and supervise actively. When you catch the puppy chewing something inappropriate, calmly redirect to an appropriate toy and praise them for chewing the right thing.

Jumping on people. Puppies jump because they want attention, and they learn it works because people typically respond (even if the response is pushing them away or saying "no," which is still attention). Instead, turn your back and ignore the puppy completely when they jump. The moment all four paws are on the floor, immediately reward with attention and treats. Ask guests to do the same. Consistency from everyone the puppy interacts with is essential.

Excessive barking. Identify the trigger. Puppies bark from boredom, excitement, anxiety, attention-seeking, and alert barking at sounds or movement. For attention-seeking barking, ignore it completely and reward silence. For alert barking, acknowledge the trigger calmly ("thank you") and redirect the puppy's attention to a toy or command. For boredom barking, increase exercise, mental stimulation, and enrichment. A tired puppy is a quiet puppy.

Separation anxiety. Puppies are social animals and being alone is genuinely stressful for them initially. Build alone time gradually. Leave the puppy in their crate with a stuffed Kong while you are in another room for 5 minutes, then 10 minutes, then 30 minutes. Keep departures and arrivals low-key. Do not make a big production of leaving or returning. Gradually the puppy learns that your departure is temporary and predicts your return.

Resource guarding. If your puppy growls, stiffens, or snaps when you approach their food bowl or a valued item, this is resource guarding and requires specific handling. Do not punish the growl (the growl is a warning; removing it does not remove the underlying anxiety, just the warning signal). Instead, approach the food bowl and drop an even higher-value treat into it. Walk past and toss a treat near the puppy. The puppy learns that your approach predicts better things, not loss. If resource guarding is severe, consult a certified professional dog trainer or veterinary behaviorist.

When to Hire a Professional Trainer

While most puppy training can be done at home, certain situations benefit from professional guidance. Consider hiring a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT-KA) if your puppy shows severe resource guarding, fear-based aggression toward people or other dogs, extreme separation anxiety that does not improve with gradual training, if you feel overwhelmed or inconsistent with training, or if you want structured socialization opportunities through puppy classes.

Group puppy classes (typically $100 to $200 for a 4 to 6 week series) offer structured socialization with other puppies and guidance from a professional trainer. Private in-home training sessions ($75 to $200 per hour) address specific behavioral issues in your home environment. Always verify that any trainer you hire uses positive reinforcement methods and holds a recognized certification.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age should you start training a puppy?

Start the day you bring your puppy home, typically at 8 weeks old. Puppies learn from their environment immediately, so early training establishes good habits before bad ones develop. Begin with house training, name recognition, and sit. Formal puppy classes can start at 8 weeks with proof of first vaccinations. The critical socialization window of 3 to 16 weeks is the most important period for shaping your puppy's lifelong temperament.

How long does it take to house train a puppy?

Most puppies achieve reliable house training in 4 to 6 months with consistent effort. Some learn the basics in 2 to 3 weeks while others, especially smaller breeds, may take up to a year. Keys to speed: take the puppy outside every 1 to 2 hours, reward immediately after outdoor elimination, supervise constantly indoors, use the crate when you cannot supervise, and clean accidents with enzymatic cleaner. Never punish accidents after the fact.

Is crate training cruel?

No. Dogs are naturally den animals that seek enclosed, secure spaces. A properly introduced crate becomes your puppy's safe space where they rest voluntarily. The crate should be appropriately sized, never used as punishment, and the puppy should never be crated longer than their age in months plus one hour during the day. The American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior supports crate training as a humane management tool when used correctly.

What are the first commands to teach a puppy?

Start with sit (easiest to learn), then come (most important safety command), stay (impulse control), down (calm behavior), and leave it (prevents eating dangerous items). Teach one command at a time in 3 to 5 minute sessions, several times daily. Always use positive reinforcement with treats and praise. Once a command is reliable at home, gradually practice in more distracting environments.

How do I stop my puppy from biting?

When bitten, say "ouch!" in a high-pitched voice and stop all interaction for 10 to 15 seconds. Resume play and redirect to a chew toy. If biting continues, leave the room for 30 seconds. This teaches that hard biting ends all fun. Be consistent. Every family member must respond identically. Provide plenty of appropriate chew outlets, especially during teething (3 to 6 months). Most puppies significantly reduce biting by 5 to 6 months as adult teeth come in.

Should I use treats or praise for training?

Start with treats for fastest results. Use small, soft, high-value treats that the puppy can eat in one second. Always pair treats with verbal praise so the puppy learns that praise predicts good things. Over weeks and months, gradually reduce treat frequency and rely more on praise, play, and real-life rewards. This transition from continuous to intermittent reinforcement actually strengthens learned behaviors long-term.

Final Thoughts

Training a puppy at home is entirely achievable with patience, consistency, and the right approach. Every well-behaved adult dog was once a mouthy, accident-prone, furniture-chewing puppy whose owner invested time in training. The effort you put in during the first year pays dividends for the next 10 to 15 years of your dog's life.

Remember the core principles: use positive reinforcement exclusively, keep training sessions short and frequent, be consistent with your expectations and responses, and prioritize socialization during the critical 3 to 16 week window. Manage the environment to prevent unwanted behaviors (puppy-proofing, crate use) rather than relying on punishment after the fact. Celebrate small victories. Your puppy sitting reliably after one week of training is a genuine accomplishment.
